Art of War

Sun Tzu

Foreward

The Art of War is the oldest military treatise in the world, yet still applicable to many things today. This particular version is a mixture of a couple different versions of English translations of the Art of War, available on the Internet. The extra material from the Project Gutenberg work is included to help give a background and extra meaning to the book, and a different work was used to convey a smoother translation of the words that retained the meaning and fire of the original work. It is my hope that you enjoy this combined work.

Chapter One: Laying Plans

Warfare is a great matter to a nation. It is the ground of death and of life. It is the road to either safety or destruction. Therefore, it must be examined. The art of war is governed by five constant factors, which must be considered in order to determine the conditions obtaining in the field. They are (1) Way, (2) Heaven, (3) Earth, (4) The Commander, and (5) Method.

The Way is what causes the people to have the same thinking as their superiors, so that they will follow him regardless of their lives, undismayed by any danger.* Heaven signifies night and day, cold and heat, times and seasons. Earth comprises distances, great and small; danger and security; open ground and narrow passages; the chances of life and death. The Commander is wisdom, credibility, benevolence, courage, and discipline. Method is organization, the chain of command, logistics, and the control of expenses. These five attributes should be familiar to every general: he who knows them will be victorious; he who does not shall fail.

* Tu Yu quotes Wang Tzu as saying: "Without constant practice, the officers will be nervous and undecided when mustering for battle; without constant practice, the general will be wavering and irresolute when the crisis is at hand.

Therefore, compare them by means of calculation, and determine their statuses. Which ruler has the Way? Which general has the most ability? Which has gained Heaven and Ground? On which side is discipline most rigorously enforced?* Which army is stronger, morally and physically? What officers and soldiers are trained?** Which army rewards and punishes clearly? By means of these, I know victory and defeat!

* Tu Mu alludes to the story of Ts`ao Ts`ao, who was such a strict disciplinarian that once, in accordance with his own severe regulations against injury to standing crops, he condemned himself to death for having allowed his horse to shy into a field of corn! In lieu of losing his head, he was persuaded to satisfy his sense of justice by cutting off his hair. Ts`ao Ts`ao's comment about the passage was curt: "when you lay down a law, see that it is not disobeyed; if it is disobeyed, the offender must be put to death."
** Tu Yu quotes Wang Tzu as saying: "Without constant practice, the officers will be nervous and undecided when mustering for battle. Without constant practice, the general will be wavering and irresolute when the crisis is at hand.

The general that listens to my counsel and acts upon it will conquer – keep him. A general who does not listen to my councel nor acts upon it will suffer defeat – remove him. While heeding the profit of my counsel, avail yourself also of any helpful circumstances over and beyond the ordinary rules. Force is the control of the balance of power in accordance with advantages.

All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; if active, appear inactive; if near, appear far; if far, appear near. If they have the advantage, entice them; if they are confused, take them; if they are substantial, prepare for them; if he has superior strength, evade him; if they are angry, disturb them; if they are humble, make them haughty; if they are relaxed, toil them; and if they are united, separate them. Attack where they are not prepared, appear where you are not expected. These military devices, leading to victory, must not be divulged beforehand.

The general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple before the battle is fought. The general who loses a battle makes but few calculations beforehand. Many calculations results in victory, few calculations yields no victory, then how much less so with no calculations? It is by attention to this point that I can foresee who is likely to win or lose.

Chapter Two: Cost of War

In the operations of war, where there are in the field a thousand swift chariots, as many heavy chariots, and a hundred thousand mail-clad soldiers, with provisions enough to carry them a thousand LI*, the expenditure at home and at the front, including the efforts of having guests, materials such as glue and paint, and sums spent on chariots and armor, will reach the total of a thousand ounces of silver per day. Such is the cost of raising an army of 100,000 men.

When doing battle, seek a quick victory. A lengthy battle will blunt weapons and dampen ardor. If troops lay siege to a walled city, their strength will be exhausted. If the campaign is prolonged, the nation's resources will not suffice. When weapons are blunted, ardor dampened, strength exhausted, and resources depleted, the neighboring rulers will spring up to take advantage of these complications. Then no man, however wise, will be able to avert the consequences that must ensue. Therefore, I have heard of military campaings that were clumsy but swift, but I have never seen military campaigns that were skilled but prolonged. No nation has ever benefited from extended warfare. It is only one who is thoroughly acquainted with the evils of war that can thoroughly understand the profitable way of carrying it on.

* There are 78 modern LI in a mile (1,000 LI → about 13 miles), but the length may have varied slightly since Sun Tsu's time.

Those skilled in doing battle do not raise troops twice, or transport provisions three times. Take equipment from home, but take provisions from the enemy. Then the army will be sufficient in both equipment and provisions. A nation can be impoverished by the army when it has to supply the army at great distances. When provisions are transported at a great distance, the citizens will be impoverished. Those in proximity to the army will sell goods at high prices. When goods are expensive, the citizen's wealth will be exhausted. When their wealth is exhausted, the peasantry will be afflicted with increased taxes. With this loss of substance and exhaustion of strength, the homes of the people will be stripped bare and 3/10 of their income will be dissipated; while government expenses for broken chariots, worn-out horses, breast-plates and helmets, bows and arrows, spears and shields, protective mantles, draught-oxen and heavy wagons, will amount to 4/10 of its reserves.

Different versions say the citizens spend 7/10 of their income and the government will spend 6/10 of its revenue.

Hence, a wise general will strive to forage on the enemy. One bushel of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own, and likewise a single pound of fodder is worth twenty of our own.* Killing the enemy is a matter of arousing anger in our men; taking the enemy's wealth is a matter of reward. Therefore, in chariot battles, reward the first to capture at least ten chariots. Replace the enemy's flags with our own. Mix the captured chariots with our own. Treat the captured soldiers will. This is called using the conquered foe to augment one's own strength.

* This is because twenty bushels of supplies will be consumed in the process of transporting one bushel to the front.

In war, let your great object be victory, not lengthy campaigns. Thus, let it be known that a general who understands warfare is the guardian of people's lives and the ruler of the nation's security.

Chapter Three: Planning Attacks

Generally in warfare, keeping the enemy's country whole and intact is better than destroying the nation. So, too, it is better to capture an army as a whole than to destroy it; to capture a regiment, detachment, or company entirely than to destroy them. Therefore, to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; to subjugate the enemy's army without doing battle is the highest mark of excellence. Thus, the highest form of generalship is to attack the enemy's plans, next is to attack alliances, next is to attack the army, and the worst policy is to besiege walled cities.

Laying siege to a city is only done when other options are not available. To build large protective shields, armored wagons, and make ready the necessary arms and equipment will require at least three months. To build earthen mounds against the walls will require another three months. The general, unable to control his irritation, will launch his men to the assault like swarming ants, with the result that one-third of his men are slain, while the town still remains untaken. Such are the disastrous effects of a siege.

Therefore, the skillfull leader subdues the enemy's troops without any fighting; he captures their cities without laying siege to them; he overthrows their kingdom without lengthy operations in the field. His goal must be to take the entire nation intact. Therefore, weapons will not be blunted, and gains will be intact. These are the principles of planning attacks.

It is the rule in war, if our forces are ten to the enemy's one, to surround him; if five to one, immediately attack him, if twice as numerous, to divide our army into two.* If equally matched, we can offer battle;** if weaker, be able to avoid and watch them. Therfore, a smaller army that is inflexible will be captured by a larger one.

* Being two to the enemy's one, we may use one part of our army in the regular way, and the other for some special diversion. One can meet the enemy in the front, the other from the rear. If he replies to the frontal attack, he may be crushed from behind. If to the rearword attack, he may be crushed in front.
** If the attackers and attacked are equally matched in strength, only the able general will fight.

A general is the safeguard of the nation. When this support is in place, the nation will certainly be strong. When this support is not in place, the nation will certaionly not be strong.

There are three ways in which a ruler can bring misfortune upon his army: (1) By commanding the army to advance or to retreat, being ignorant of the fact that it is in no position to obey. This is called hobbling the army. (2) By attempting to govern an amry in the same way as he administers a kingdom, being ignorant of the army's condition and matters. This causes confusion in the soldier's minds. (3) By not being careful to use the right man in the right place, through ignorance of the military principle of adaptation to circumstances. This shakes the confidence of the soldiers. When the army is confused and hesitant, the neighboring rulers will take advantage. This is simply bringing anarchy into the army, and flinging victory away.

There are five factors of knowing who will win: (1) One who knows when he can fight and when he cannot fight will be victorious. (2) One who knows how to use both large and small forces will be victorious. (3) One who knows how to unite upper and lower ranks in a singular purpose will be victorious. (4) One who is prepared and waits for the unprepared will be victorious. (5) One whose general is able and is not interfered by the ruler will be victorious. These five factors are the way to know who will win.

Hence the saying: One who knows the enemy and knows himself need not fear the result of a hundred battles. One who does not know the enemy but knows himself will sometimes win, sometimes lose. One who does not know the enemy and does not know himself will be in danger in every battle.

Chapter Four: Formation

In ancient times, those skilled kin warfare make themselves invincible and then wait for the enemy to become vulderable. To secure ourselves against defeat lies in our own hands, but the opportunity of defeating the enemy is provided by the enemy himself. Thus, the good fighter is able to secure himself against defeat,* but cannot make certain of defeating the enemy. Therefore it is said that one may know how to win but are not able to do it.

* Chang Yu says this is done, "By concealing the disposition of his troops, covering up his tracks, and taking unremitting precautions."

One takes on invincibility defending, one takes on vulnerability attacking. Standing on the defensive indicates insufficient strength; attackin, a superabundance of strength. The general who is skilled in defense hides in the most secret recesses of the earth; he who is skilled in attack falls upon his adversary like a thunderbolt, against which there is no time to prepare. On one hand, we have the ability to protect ourselves; on the other, a victory that is complete.

Perceiving a victory when it is perceived by all is not the pinnacle of excellence. Winning battles such that the whole world says "excellent" is not the greatest excellence. For lifting an autumn hair* is no sign of great strengthseeing the sun and moon is not considered a sign of sharp vision, hearing thunder is not considered a sign of sensitive hearing. What the ancients called a clever fighter is one who not only wins, but excels at winning with ease. Hence, his victories bring him neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage.** He wins his battles by making no mistakes. Making no mistakes is what establishes the certainty of victory, for it means conquering an enemy that is already defeated.

* "Autumn hair" is explained as the fur of a hare, which is finest in autumn. The phrase is a common one in Chinese writers.
** Tu Mu explains this very well: Inasmuch as his victories are gained over circumstances that have not come to light, the world at large kinows nothing of them, and he wins no reputation for wisdom; inasmuch as the hostile state submits before there has been any bloodshed, he receives no credit for courage.

Those skilled in warfare establish positions that make them invincible and do not miss opportunities to attack the enemy. Therefore, a victorious army first obtains conditions for victory, then seeks to do battle. One who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory. Those skilled in warfare cultivates the Way and strongly adheres to Method and discipline; thus it is in his power to govern victory and defeat.

The factors in warfare are measurement, quantity, calculation, comparison, and finally victory. Measurements are derived from Earth, qquantities are derived from measurement, calculations are derived from quantities, comparisons are derived from calculations, and victories are derived from comparisons. A victorious army is like a ton against an ounce; a defeated army is like an ounce against a ton! The onrush of a conquering force is like the bursting of pent-up waters into a chasm a thousand fathoms deep.

Chapter Five: Force

Commanding a large force is the same as commanding a small force. It is a matter of dividing them into groups. Doing battle with a large army is like doing battle with a small army. It is merely a question of instituting signs and signals.

What enables an army to withstand the enemy's attack and not be defeated are direct and indirect maneuvers. That the impact of your army may be like a boulder hurled at an egg; it is a matter of weakness and strength. Generally, in battle, use the direct route to engage the enemy and the indirect route to gain victory. Indirect tactics, efficiently applied, are inexhaustible as Heaven and Earth, unending as the flow of rivers and streams. There are no more than five basic colors*, yet the variations in the five colors cannot all be seen. There are no more than five basic flavors**, yet the variations in the five flavors cannot all be tasted. In battle, there are no more than two types of attacks: direct and indirect; yet these two in combination give rise to an endless series of maneuvers. The direct and the indirect produce each other, like an endless circle. Who can exhaust the possibilities of their combinations?

* Blue, yellow, red, white, black.
** Sour, acrid, salt, sweet, bitter.

The onset of troops is like the rush of torrential waters which will even roll boulders along its course; this illustrates force. The quality of decision is like the well-timed strike of a bird, which enables it to strike and destroy its victim; this illustrates timing. Therefore the force of those skilled in warfare is overwhelming and their timing is precise. Their force is like a drawn crossbow; their timing is like the release of the trigger.

Even in the midst of the turbulence of battle, the fighting seemingly chaotic and yet no real disorder at all; amid confusion and chaos, your array may appear to be without a head or tail, yet it will be undefeatable. Simulated disorder requires perfect discipline, simulated fear requires courage, simulated weakness requires strength.* Hiding order beneath the cloak of disorder is simply a matter of organization, concealing courage under a show of timidity is a matter of force, masking strength with weakness is a matter of formation.

* Tu Mu puts this quite plainly: "If you wish to feign confusion in order to lure the enemy on, you first must have perfect discipline; if you wish to display timidity in order to entrap the enemy, you must have extreme courage; if you wish to parade your weakness in order to make the enemy over-confident, you must have exceeding strength."

Therefore, those skilled in moving the enemy use formations to which the enemy must respond. They offer bait that the enemy must take, manipulating the enemy to move while they wait in ambush. The clever combatant uses the effect of combined energy, and does not require too much from individuals. Hence his ability to pick out the right men and utilize combined energy. One who exploits this type of force commands men into battle like rolling logs and buoulders. Logs and boulders are still when on flat ground, but roll when on steep ground. Square shapes are still, but round shapes roll. Thus the energy developed by good fighting men is as the momentum of a round stone rolled down a mountain thousands of feet in height. So much on the subject of force.

Chapter Six: Weakness and Strength

Whoever is first in the battlefield and waits for the enemy is at ease; the one who comes later and rushes into battle is fatigued. Therefore, the clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy and are not moved by the enemy.* By holding out advantages to him, he can cause the enemy to approach of his own accord; stopping the enemy from approaching is a matter of showing him harm.

* One mark of a great soldier is that he fights on his own terms or doesn't fight at all.

If the enemy is at ease, be able to exhaust him; if the enemy is well fed, be able to starve him; if the enemy is settled, be able to move him. Appear at places where he must rush to defend, and rush to places where he least expects. An army can march great distances without becoming distressed where the enemy is not present. You can be certain of success in your attacks if you only attack weak points. You can be certain of the safety of your defense if you only hold positions that contain no weak points.* Therefore, against those skilled in attack, the enemy does not know where to defend; against those skilled in defense, the enemy does not know where to attack. O divine art of subtlety and secrecy! Through you we learn to be without form or sound; and hence we can hold the enemy's fate in our hands.

* Chang Yu expand on this: "He who is skilled in attack flashes forth from the topmost heights of Heaven, making it impossible for the enemy to guard against him. This being so, the places that I shall attack are precisely those that the enemy cannot defend. He who is skilled in defense hides in the most secret recesses of the Earth, making it impossible for the enemy to estimate his whereabouts. This being so, the places that I shall hold are precisely those that the enemy cannot attack."

To achieve an advance that cannot be hampered, rush to his weak points. To achieve a withdrawl that cannot be pursued, depart with superior speed. If we want to do battle, even if the enemy is sheltered behind high walls and deep moats, he is unable to not do battle. We attack what he must rescue.* If we do not want to do battle, even if we merely draw a line on the ground, he will not do battle. All we need to do is throw something odd and unaccountable in his way.**

* Tun My says: "If the enemy is the invading party, we can cut his line of communications and occupy the roads by which he will have to return; if we are the invaders, we may direct are attack against the soverign himself."
** Tu Mu clinches this meaning with an illustrative anecdote of Chu-ko Liang. When he was occupying Yang-p`ing and was about to be attacked by Ssu-ma I, he suddenly struck his colors, stopped the beating of the drums, and flung open the city gates. Inside appeared only a few men engaged in sweeping and sprinkling the ground. This unexpected proceeding had the intended effect; for Ssu-ma I, suspecting an ambush, actually drew off his army and retreated. What Sun Tsu is advocating here, therefore, is nothing more nor less than the timely use of bluff.

Therefore, if we can make the enemy show his position while remaining invisible ourselves, we will be at full force when the enemy is divided to protect against attack from every quarter. We can form a single united body, while the enemy must split up into fractions and we will attack him at ten times his strength. Hence, there will be a whole pitted against separate parts of a whole, which means that we shall be many to the enemy's few. If we are able thus to attack an inferior force with a superior one, our opponents will be in dire straits.

The place of battle must not be made known to the enemy. If it is not known, then the enemy must prepare to defend many places. If he prepares to defend many places, then the forces will be few in number. Therefore, if he prepares to defend the front, the back will be weak. If he prepares to defend the back, the front will be weak. If he strengthens his left, the right will be weak. If he strengthens his right, the left will be weak. If he prepares to defend everywhere, everywhere will be weak.* Numerical weakness comes from having to prepare against possible attacks; numerical strength comes from compelling our adversary to make these preparations against us.

* In Frederick the Great's Instructions To His Generals we read: "A defensive war is apt to betray us into too frequent detachment. Those generals who have had but little experience attempt to protect every point, while those who are better acquainted with their profession, having only the capital object in view, guard against a decisive blow, and acquiesce in small misfortunes to avoid greater."

Knowing the place and the time of the coming battle, we may concentrate from the greatest distances in order to fight. If one does not know the place of battle and the day of battle, then his left cannot aid his right and his right cannot aid his left; his front unable to relieve the rear and the rear unable to support the front. How much more so if the furthest portions of the army are separated by tens of miles or hundreds of miles. Based on my calculations, though the soldiers of Yueh exceed our own in number, that has no advantage in respect to victory. Therefore I say victory can be achieved.

Though the enemy is stronger in numbers, we may prevent him from fighting. Know the enemy's plans and calculate their strengths and weaknesses. Provoke him and to learn his patterns of movement. Determine his position in order to find out his vulnerable spots. Carefully compare the opposing army with your own, so that you may know where strength is superabundant and where it is deficient.

The ultimate skill is to take up a position where you show no sign of what you mean to do. If you are able to hide your intentions, you will be safe from the most penetrating spies and from the calculations of the wisest counsels. With formation, the army achieves victories out of the enemy's own tactics, yet they do not understand how. Everyone knows the formation by which you achieved victory, yet no one knows the formations by which you were able to create victory. Do not repeat the tactics which have gained you one fictory, but let your methods be regulated by the infinite variety of circumstances.

Military tactics are like water. The water's formation avoids the high and rushes to the low. So in war, the way is to avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak. Water's formation adapts to the ground when flowing. So then an army's formation adapts to the enemy to achieve victory. Therefore, just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions. Those who are able to adapt and change in accord with the enemy and achieve victory are called divine. Therefore, of the five elements, none has a constant victor, of the four seasons, none has constant position; the sun has short and long spans, and the moon waxes and wanes.

Chapter Seven: Armed Struggle

In war, the general receives his commands from the ruler, collects his army, and must establish harmony and confidence between higher and lower ranks before venturing into the field. Tactical maneuvering comes after that, and nothing is more difficult. The challenge is turing the devius into the direct and misfortune into advantage. Therefore, if you make the enemy's route circuitous and bait him with advantages, though you start behind him, you will arrive before him. This shows knowledge of the artifice of deviation.

Maneuvering with an army is advantageous, but is most dangerous with an undisciplined multitude. If you set a fully equipped army to march in order to snatch an advantage, the chances are that you will be too late. If a reduced army is sent, your stores and equipment will be lost. Thus, if you roll up your armor and make forced marches without halting day or night, covering double the usual distance for an advantage a hundred miles, the leaders of your divisions will fall into the hands of the enemy. The stronger men will arrive first, the weak ones will fall behind, and only one-tenth of your army will reach its destination.* If one struggles for a distance that is fifty miles away, the leader of your front forces will be lost and only one-half will arrive. If one struggles for an advantage thirty miles away, two-thirds of your army will arrive. For this reason, if an army is without its equipment, it will lose; if an army is without its provisions, it will lose; if the army is without its stores, it will lose.

* The moral is: Don't march a hundred miles to gain a tactical advantage, either with or without impedimenta. Maneuvers of this type should be confied to short distances. The hardships of forced marches are often more painful than the dangers of battle. Only when great surprise or a rapid retreat is imperative, one should sacrifice everything for speed.

One who does not know the intentions of the rulers of the neighboring states cannot secure alliances. One who does not know the mountains and forests, mountains and valleys, swamps and wetlands cannot advance the army. One who does not use local guides cannot take advantage of the ground.

The army that practices deception will succeed. Curcumstances must dictate whether to concentrate or to divide your troops. Your army will advance rapidly and without tracks like the wind, march with density* like the forest, invades and plunders like a blazing fire, is as immovable like the mountain, is formless like the dark, strikes like lightning and thunder. When you plunder the countryside, divide the wealth among your troops. When you expand your territory, divide up and hold places of advantage. Calculate the situation before you make a move. Those who know the art of deception will be victorious. Such is the art of maneuvering.

* While a forest doesn't have order and ranks, which must be preserved, it does have the quality of density or compactness. This guards against surprise attacks.

From the Book on Army Management: The spoken word does not carry in battle, thus drums and gongs are used. Troops cannot see each other clearly, thus flags and pennants are used. These tools unite the army into a single united body where the brave cannot advance alone and the cowardly cannot retreat alone. This is the art of handling large masses of men. In night battles, use many torches and drums. In day battles, use many flags and pennants in order to influence men's eyes and ears.

A whole army may be robbed of its spirit, a general can be robbed of his presence of mind. A soldier's spirit is keenest in the morning; by noonday, it has begun to wane; and in the evening, his mind is only set on returning to camp. A clever general avoids an army when its energy is up, but attacks when it is sluggish and inclined to return. This is the art of studying moods.

Await the appearance of disorder and clamoramong the enemy while remaining disciplined and calm yourself. This is the way to manage the mind. Be near the goal while the enemy is still far from it, then rest while the enemy is toiling and struggling; be well fed when the enemy is famished. This is the art of managing strength. Refrain from intercepting an enemy whose banners are in perfect order; do not not battle with well-regulated formations. This is the art of studying circumstances. The principles of warfare are to not attack an enemy that has the high ground, do not attack an enemy that has his back to a hill, do not persue feigned retreats, do not attack elite troops, do not swallow the enemy's bait, do not thwart an enemy retreating home*. If you surround the enemy, leave a path of retreat**. Do not press an enemy that is cornered or a foe that is desperate. Such is the art of warfare.

* A man whose heart is set on returning home will fight to the death against any attempt ot bar his way and is therefore too dangerous an opponent to be tackled. Han Hsin said, "Invincible is the soldier who hath his desire and returneth homewards."

Chapter Eight: Variation In Tactics

In war, the general receives his commands from the ruler, assembles the armies, and mobilizes the masses. Do not camp on difficult ground. Unite with your allies on intersecting ground. Do not stay on open ground. Be prepared on surrounded ground. Do battle on deadly ground. There are roads which must not be followed, armies which must not be attacked, towns which must not be besieged, grounds which must not be contested, commands which must not be obeyed.

The general who thoroughly understands the advantages that varying tactics knows how to handle his troops. The general who does not understand these, even if he knows the lay of the land, will not be able to use his knowledge to get the advantage of the ground. Those who command an army but does not understand variations in tactics, even if he is familiar with a basic understanding of warfare, will not be able to best use his troops.

Therefore, the wise general contemplates both the advantages and disadvantages. If our expectation of advantage is tempered in this way, we may succeed in accomplishing the critical part of our schemes. If we are always ready to sieze an advantage in the midst of difficulties, we may extricate ourselves from misfortune. Get the enemy into a position where he must suffer injury, and he will submit of his own accord. Make trouble for the neighboring rulers with constant matters, and prevent them from having any rest. Hold out specious allurements, and make them rush to any given point.

The art of war teaches us to not rely on the likelihood of the enemy's not coming, but on our own readiness to receive him; not on the chance of his not attacking, but rather on the fact htat we have made our position unassailable. There are five dangerous traits of a general: Recklessness, which leads to destruction; cowardice, which leads to capture; tempermental, which can be provoked by insults; a delicacy of honor, which is sensitive to shame; being too fond of his men, which exposes him to worry and trouble. These five traits are faults in a general and are disastrous in warfare. The army's destruction and the death of the general are due to these five dangerous traits. Let them be a subject of meditation.

Chapter Nine: Army Maneuvers

One must consider how to position the army and observe the signs of the enemy. Pass quickly over mountains and keep in the neighborhood of valleys. Camp in high places, facing the sun. If the enemy holds the high ground, do not ascend and do battle with him. So much for mountain warfare.

After crossing a river, you should get far away from it. If the enemy crosses a river, do not meet him in the water. It will be best to let half the army get across, and then deliver your attack. If you are anxious to fight, you should not go to meet the invader near a river which he has to cross. Moor your craft higher up than the enemy and facing the sun. Do not move upstream to meet the enemy. So much for river warfare.

After crossing swamps and wetlands, stive to quickly get through them; do not linger. If you do battle in swamps and wetlands, you must position close to grass, with the trees to your back. So much for marshland warfare.

In dry, level country, take up an easily accessible position with your right backed by high ground, the dangerous ground in front, and safe ground to the back so that the danger may be in front and safety lie behind. So much for grassland warfare. These are the four positions advantageous to the army, which enabled the Yellow Emperor to vanquish four soverigns.

All armies prefer high ground to low, and sunny places to dark. If you are careful of your men and camp on hard ground, the army will be free from disease of every kind, and this will spell victory. When there are hills and embankments, occupy the sunny side with the slope on your right rear. Thus you will at once act for the benefit of your soldiers and utilize the natural advantages of the ground.

When the rainwater up-country swells a river which you wish to ford, wait until it settles. Where there is ground with precipitous cliffs, impassable ravines, natural hollows, confined places, tangled thickets, quagmires, and crevasses, it should be left with all possible speed and not approached. While we avoid such places, we should get the enemy to approach them. When we face the enemy, he will have such places at his back. When the army is flanked by high ground, wetlands, tall reeds and grass, mountain forests, or areas thick with undergrowth, they must be carefully examined and searched because these are places where men lie in ambush or where spies hide.
